Albert Einstein

Born March 14, 1879, Ulm, Württemberg, Germany—died April 18, 1955, Princeton, New Jersey, U.S. German-born physicist who developed the special and general theories of relativity and won the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1921 for his explanation of the photoelectric effect. Childhood and Education

Albert Einstein's childhood and early life set the stage for his future achievements. His unconventional thinking, innate curiosity, and early passion for science laid the foundation for the groundbreaking theories and discoveries that would later define his career in physics.

From graduation to the “miracle year” of scientific theories of Albert Einstein

After graduation in 1900, Einstein faced one of the greatest crises in his life. Because he studied advanced subjects on his own, he often cut classes; this earned him the animosity of some professors, especially Heinrich Weber. Unfortunately, Einstein asked Weber for a letter of recommendation.

Personal life

Einstein had a complicated personal life, marked by his intense focus on scientific pursuits and his unconventional approach to relationships. While he made significant contributions to the field of physics, his personal life was not without challenges, including strained relationships with some family members.

Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) Paradox (1935)

In collaboration with colleagues Podolsky and Rosen, Einstein published a paper highlighting the peculiar features of quantum entanglement, challenging aspects of quantum mechanics. The EPR paradox became a foundational element in the study of quantum entanglement.

Cosmology

Einstein made contributions to cosmology, including his introduction of the cosmological constant to his equations to achieve a static universe. He later referred to this as his "greatest blunder" when evidence for the expanding universe emerged.
