From graduation to the “miracle year” of scientific theories of Albert Einstein

Meanwhile, Einstein’s relationship with Maric deepened, but his parents vehemently opposed the relationship. His mother especially objected to her Serbian background (Maric’s family was Eastern Orthodox Christian). Einstein defied his parents, however, and in January 1902 he and Maric even had a child, Lieserl, whose fate is unknown. (It is commonly thought that she died of scarlet fever or was given up for adoption.)

After Albert Einstein graduated from the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology (ETH) in Zurich in 1900, he faced challenges in finding suitable employment within academia. Initially, he worked as a substitute teacher and a private tutor while submitting research papers to scientific journals. However, his breakthrough came in what is now referred to as the "miracle year" of 1905, during which he published four groundbreaking papers that fundamentally altered the course of physics:

Photoelectric Effect (March 1905): Einstein's paper on the photoelectric effect proposed that light could be thought of as quanta of energy, or particles later termed photons. This work laid the foundation for the development of quantum theory and earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921. Brownian Motion (May 1905): Einstein's work on Brownian motion provided convincing evidence for the existence of atoms and molecules. His statistical analysis of the erratic motion of small particles suspended in a fluid supported the kinetic theory of matter. Special Theory of Relativity (June 1905): Einstein's paper on the special theory of relativity challenged classical notions of space and time.
